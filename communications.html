COMMUNICATION


From ancient smoke plumes and sun signals to the mobile telephone, communications have been revolutionized by technology.



The long timeline of long-distance communication


At the start, around 1300BC...
It was believed the Egyptians would use carrier pigeons to send messages.
From 900BC onwards
Smoke signals were used along the great wall of China as warnings of invasion.
Then after a long break filled with messengers and carrier pigeons, in the 1790s

The Chappe telegraph was created by Claude Chappe. His system featured many different towers that could diplay messages via semaphore arms that could be viewed via telescope.

This system was far more efficient than anything seen before. Messages with detail could be sent over long distances.

There were issues with this system, however, as the messages were not very private, being visible to anybody nearby with a telescope. They were also very physically demanding to operate, especially as words were sent whole and not abbreviated for speed.

In 1837

William Cooke and Charles Wheatstone patented an electric telegraphing system with 5 needles and 6 wires, using needle orientations to correspond to letters of the alphabet.

This invention was used frequently in railway signalling, which greatly improved the coordination and safety of the network.

Strangely enough, this system was actually used to catch a murderer! John Tawell, a suspect of the murder of Sarah Hart, boarded a train from Slough to Paddington. Police warned railway officers in Paddington via the telegraph and he was caught shortly afterwards.

At the same time

Samuel Morse and Alfred Vail created Morse code, another electric telegraphing system. This was a very impactful invention, being used throughout World War 2 for long distance communication.
